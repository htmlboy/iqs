UX & IA

Design terms

Usability

The ease of use and learnability of an interface (or an object).

UX: User Experience

A person's emotions and attitudes about using a particular product, system or service.

The knowledge, techniques and tools interaction designers use to create a good experience.

IA: Information Architecture

The art and science of organizing and labelling websites, intranets, online communities and software to support usability and findability.

Research

Briefing

Define the problem and its context with a questionnaire or during a meeting.

Define the scope, goals, deadline, budget, target, market, strengths, current problems and strategy.

Common UX methodologies

  • Observation, Understanding, Analysis
  • Interviews
  • Surveys and questionnaires
  • Usability tests
  • A/B Tests
  • Card Sorting
  • Tree Tests

Card Sorting

In a card sort, a user is provided with a set of terms, and asked to categorize them. In a closed card sort, the user is also given the category names; in an open card sort the user creates whatever categories they feel are most appropriate.

Card Sorting

The goal of a card sort is to explore relationships between content, and better understand the hierarchies that a user perceives. Many content strategists and information architects rely on card sorts to test out hierarchy theories, or kickstart work on a site map.

Tree Tests

Users are given a task and shown the top level of a site map. Then they are asked to talk through where they would go to accomplish the task. However, the user doesn’t see a screen when they choose a site section. Instead, they will see the next level of the architecture.

Tree Tests

The goal is to identify whether information is categorized correctly and how appropriately the nomenclature reflects the sections of the site.

Gather content

A content inventory is the process and the result of cataloging the entire contents of a website. An allied practice —a content audit— is the process of evaluating that content.

Content inventory

A content inventory typically includes all information assets on a website, such as web pages (html), meta elements (e.g., keywords, description, page title), images, audio and video files, and document files.

Content inventory

A content inventory is a quantitative analysis of a website. It simply logs what is on a website. The content inventory will answer the question: “What is there?” and can be the start of a website review.

Content audit

A content audit is an accounting of all currently published web content. It is a qualitative analysis of information assets on a website.

Content audit can answer five questions: What content do we already have?; Who is making this content?; How do people find it?; How is it performing?; and Is the content current (accurate) or outdated?

Prioritise content

After the content audit, the content list is ordered by priority. This prioritisation will lead the information architecture and visual design.

The card sorting technique is sometimes used here to rearrange the content in an intuitive way.

Sketch some wireframes

Wireframes

Wireframes are schematical draws of a website. They can be done on paper or with design software, and can be very rough or detailed, depending on the needs of the design process.

They focus on information arrangement and are helpful for quickly share ideas with team members and clients. They will lead visual design and development.

Tools

  • Pencil, paper and post-it notes
  • A spreadsheet (helpful for content inventories)
  • Trello (task and issue manager, but also helpful for card sorting and content prioritisation).
  • Mockflow (online wireframing tool)
